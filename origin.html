Benz Patent-Motorwagen (1886)

Karl Benz’s Patent-Motorwagen is widely regarded as the first practical automobile. It used a single-cylinder four-stroke engine mounted horizontally at the rear, a tubular steel frame, and three wire-spoked wheels. Bertha Benz’s famous long-distance drive in 1888 demonstrated the car’s reliability and birthed the concept of fuel stations and roadside maintenance.

Early DMG & “Mercedes” (1890s–1900s)

Meanwhile, DMG (Daimler-Motoren-Gesellschaft) produced fast, lightweight cars with engines by Gottlieb Daimler and Wilhelm Maybach. The name “Mercedes” became associated with success after cars were commissioned by businessman Emil Jellinek for racing — named after his daughter Mercédès.

1926: Daimler-Benz AG and the Three-Pointed Star

The merger of Benz & Cie. and DMG in 1926 formed Daimler-Benz AG. The unified cars used the three-pointed star emblem and the brand name Mercedes-Benz, symbolizing power on land, sea, and air.
